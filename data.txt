[RESEARCH]
ID: semantic-confusion
TITLE: When Safety Blocks Sense: Semantic Confusion in LLM Refusals
ROLE: Co-author
DATE: Dec 2025
VENUE: arXiv:2512.01037
FEATURED: yes
LINK: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.01037
TAGS: LLM safety, alignment, evaluation
SUMMARY: Identified a failure mode where LLMs reject harmless paraphrases of intents they otherwise accept, and built a corpus and metrics to measure it.
PROBLEM: Safety-aligned language models often refuse requests inconsistently: a model may accept one phrasing of a harmless intent while rejecting a near-identical paraphrase. We named this failure mode "Semantic Confusion" — local inconsistencies in refusals that reveal gaps in how alignment generalizes across surface form.
CONTRIBUTION: As co-author, I helped formalize the failure mode and build the tools to study it. We introduced ParaGuard, a 10,000-prompt corpus of controlled paraphrase clusters, and proposed three token-level metrics — Confusion Index, Confusion Rate, and Confusion Depth — to diagnose where and how strongly models become inconsistent.
METHOD: The metrics combine sentence embeddings (to confirm paraphrases are semantically equivalent) with perplexity and token-level signals (to locate where the model's decision flips). This lets us separate genuine safety boundaries from brittle, surface-level pattern matching.
RESULTS: ParaGuard surfaces measurable inconsistency across models, turning a previously anecdotal "the model is being weird" observation into a quantifiable, comparable signal. The framework gives a concrete handle on a real alignment gap.
REFLECTION: I'd like to extend this toward intervention: can we use Confusion metrics during fine-tuning to actively reduce inconsistency, and do these gaps correlate with other robustness failures? The broader question — how alignment generalizes across semantically equivalent inputs — is central to where I want to take my PhD research.

[RESEARCH]
ID: crowd-tracking-loss
TITLE: A Contextually Appropriate Loss Function for Dense Crowd Tracking
ROLE: Undergraduate thesis · Supervisor: Prof. A. B. M. Alim Al Islam (CSE, BUET)
DATE: Feb 2024 – Mar 2025
VENUE: BSc Thesis, BUET
FEATURED: no
LINK:
TAGS: computer vision, loss design, object tracking
SUMMARY: Designed a size-aware, gradient-stabilized loss function that outperforms standard IoU losses for tracking small, overlapping objects in dense crowds.
PROBLEM: Tracking small, heavily overlapping objects in dense crowds is hard: standard bounding-box losses (CIoU, DIoU, GIoU) treat large and tiny objects similarly and produce unstable gradients for the small, clustered targets that dominate crowd scenes.
CONTRIBUTION: I proposed a size-aware, gradient-stabilized loss function tailored to small overlapping objects, and integrated it into YOLO-based detection and tracking models as the focus of my undergraduate thesis.
METHOD: The loss reweights contributions by object scale and stabilizes gradients to prevent small targets from being drowned out during optimization, improving learning on the long tail of tiny instances.
RESULTS: Evaluated on JHU-CROWD++ and SCUT-HEAD, the loss achieved superior mAP@50 and F1 scores compared to CIoU, DIoU, and GIoU baselines.
REFLECTION: This work taught me that the loss function is often where the real modeling decisions live — a theme that carries directly into my interest in how we define and measure objectives for ML systems.
